Age guide · 12–24 months

The first compounding: toddlerhood & executive function

This is the flagship band. Walking arrives, first words arrive, and so does the very first, tiniest version of self-control. Every lesson here is tagged to one of six executive-function skills — so you can see exactly what a five-minute game is actually building.

TL;DR
  • Every 12–24 month lesson carries a pillar tag (focus, impulse control, working memory, flexibility, self-regulation, or delayed gratification) and a one-line "compounds into" payoff.
  • Freeze dance is the single best impulse-control game at this age — stopping on cue, joyfully, over and over.
  • The tiny wait (2–3 seconds before a snack) is the toddler-scale ancestor of the marshmallow test — and it's trainable.
  • When a shape sorter or puzzle frustrates them, the struggle is the lesson — a slower parent, not a faster fix, is what builds flexibility.
  • Curated video (Ms. Rachel and similar) is used deliberately and co-viewed, not avoided or used as a babysitter.

Five activities, five different executive-function skills

Freeze dance (stop & go)

Impulse Control · 8 min

Play music, dance, then suddenly stop it — "FREEZE!" Start again. Gradually make the freezes longer to raise the challenge.

  • Stop-and-go games directly train inhibitory control — the core of executive function.
  • It's joyful, so toddlers build the hardest skill while laughing.
Compounds into: the pause between impulse and action — the trait that, measured at age 4, predicts decades of outcomes. Cited from: Diamond, A. (2013) on executive function; Tools of the Mind research on self-regulation games.

The tiny wait

Delayed Gratification · 5 min

Snack in hand: "wait… wait… NOW." Start with just 2 seconds and stretch slowly, always honoring the wait.

  • Waiting is trainable — start with 2–3 seconds and build gradually.
  • Trust that the reward comes is what makes longer waiting possible later.
Compounds into: the literal psychology of wealth — choosing the bigger later over the smaller now. Cited from: Mischel's delayed-gratification research and its decades-long predictive power.

Shape sorter — let them struggle

Flexibility · 8 min

Drop blocks into a box with a cut hole. Don't rush to help — count to ten before stepping in.

  • The struggle IS the lesson — jumping in to solve it robs them of the flexibility rep.
  • Celebrate the effort and strategy ("you turned it!"), not just the success.
Compounds into: the resilience and problem-solving that separates people who quit from people who figure it out. Cited from: Dweck on process praise; cognitive-flexibility development research.

Pouring & transferring

Focus · 8 min

Two small cups plus dry beans or water. Expect spills — that's fine, the spilling and noticing is part of the learning.

  • The intense focus toddlers bring to pouring is concentration being trained.
  • A small sponge nearby lets them own the cleanup too.
Compounds into: sustained focus and the dignity of doing real things well. Cited from: Montessori practical-life research on concentration.

Toys-away ritual

Impulse Control · 6 min

One basket, one cleanup song, every night, done together — never as punishment.

  • Stopping fun to put things back is a real inhibitory-control rep, done daily.
  • Doing it WITH them builds the identity of someone who contributes, not just consumes.
Compounds into: the discipline of finishing — and the identity of a contributor. Cited from: Inhibitory-control research; family-contribution studies (Rossmann).

Frequently asked

What is the best impulse-control game for a toddler?
Freeze dance. Play music, dance, then suddenly stop and call "FREEZE!" Stop-and-go games directly train inhibitory control — the core of executive function — while staying joyful, so toddlers build one of the hardest self-regulation skills while laughing. Gradually holding the freeze longer raises the challenge.
How do you teach a toddler to wait for something?
Start absurdly small: hold a snack and say "wait… wait… NOW" after just 2–3 seconds, then reliably deliver it. Waiting is trainable in tiny increments, and always honoring the wait is what builds the trust that makes longer waiting possible later — the toddler ancestor of the marshmallow test.
Should I help my toddler when a puzzle or shape sorter frustrates them?
Not immediately. The struggle — trying, failing, rotating, and finally succeeding — is cognitive flexibility being built in real time. A common guideline is to count to ten before stepping in, and to praise the process ("you turned it!") rather than just the success.
Is co-viewing children's videos actually better than letting a toddler watch alone?
Yes. Watching with your toddler and repeating words or signs back roughly doubles language uptake compared to solo viewing, according to AAP media guidance. The screen becomes an assistant to the interaction rather than a replacement for it.
