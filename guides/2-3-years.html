Age guide · 2–3 years

The language explosion, day by day

Vocabulary, real practical-life work, curated movement, and naming big feelings — here's exactly what Dad Village schedules for 2–3 year olds in Week 1, and the research behind each piece.

TL;DR
  • The same morning sign-language lesson repeats daily for two weeks on purpose — receptive language runs about 6 months ahead of expressive language at this age.
  • Real chores beat pretend play: pouring water and wiping tables measurably lower behavior-disruption because the child feels useful, not entertained.
  • Reading aloud daily (not weekly) is linked to roughly 1.4 million more words heard by kindergarten.
  • Curated videos (counting songs, movement breaks, feelings-naming) are scheduled deliberately by skill, not left to autoplay.
  • Weekends shift toward more unstructured 1:1 dad time and longer outdoor exploration.

The anchor lessons of Week 1

Morning greeting + first signs

Repeats daily · 12 min

Dad signs Hello / More / All done at breakfast, alongside a short co-viewed video. The exact same lesson runs every morning of the week.

  • Receptive language develops about 6 months ahead of expressive — signs unlock communication before speech is fully ready.
  • Predictable morning routines lower cortisol and increase attention for the rest of the day.
  • Co-viewing (watching WITH them, naming things back) doubles the language uptake versus solo viewing.
Cited from: Acredolo & Goodwyn (2000), Pediatric Research, on baby signs; AAP 2024 Media Use guidance on co-viewing.

Pouring station

Practical Life · 8 min

Two small pitchers plus dry rice or water at the table. Real work, not pretend — done by them, not for them.

  • Fine-motor pincer and wrist-rotation work correlates with later handwriting readiness.
  • Real work drops behavior-disruption rates in this age band because they feel useful, not entertained.
  • The same task daily for two weeks beats novel tasks — this is the foundation of focused attention.
Cited from: Lillard, A. (2017) Montessori: The Science Behind the Genius, Ch. 4 on practical life.

Movement break — full body

Motor · 10 min · Alternating days

A curated cross-body movement video after lunch, or 5 minutes of animal walks around the living room if you skip the screen.

  • Cross-lateral movement (left arm + right leg, etc.) activates corpus-callosum traffic — a measurable focus boost lasting 30–45 minutes.
  • Vestibular input (spinning, jumping) regulates an over-stimulated toddler's nervous system better than a time-out.
  • Alternating-day cadence keeps novelty high — daily would become background noise.
Cited from: Hannaford, C. (2005) Smart Moves: Why Learning Is Not All in Your Head.

Read aloud with Dad

Bonding · 12 min · Daily

Any board book, sitting on the floor, phone in another room. The highest-leverage twelve minutes of the day.

  • Children read to daily hear roughly 1.4 million more words by kindergarten than children read to only weekly.
  • Dad reading specifically shows a stronger correlation with later academic engagement.
  • A phone in the room — even face-down — measurably reduces conversational turn-taking.
Cited from: Logan, J. et al. (2019) The Million Word Gap, Journal of Developmental & Behavioral Pediatrics.

Naming feelings

Emotional · 14 min · Twice a week

Curated content built specifically for naming feelings, or simply Dad naming his own feelings out loud during dinner.

  • Giving feelings names is the foundation of self-regulation.
  • Modeling emotional narration ("I'm feeling frustrated, so I'm taking a breath") teaches the skill by example.

Frequently asked

Why does Dad Village schedule the exact same sign-language video every morning?
Because receptive language develops about 6 months ahead of expressive language at this age — signs unlock communication before speech is fully ready, and predictable morning routines lower cortisol and increase attention for the rest of the day. Repeating the same short lesson for two weeks straight is the point, not a bug.
Is "pretend" chore play as valuable as real chores for a 2-3 year old?
No — real work outperforms pretend play at this age. Giving a 2-3 year old a genuine, controlled task like pouring water or wiping a table measurably drops behavior-disruption rates, because they feel useful and capable rather than merely entertained. This is a core Montessori practical-life principle.
How much does reading aloud actually matter at age 2-3?
A great deal. Children read to daily by a parent hear roughly 1.4 million more words by kindergarten than children read to only weekly. Reading the same book three nights in a row is recommended — repetition is where the vocabulary actually lands, and having a phone in the room, even face-down, measurably reduces conversational turn-taking.
What is the best way to help a 2-3 year old name their emotions?
Giving feelings names out loud — either through content built for it or simply by narrating your own emotions during the day ("I'm feeling frustrated, so I'm taking a breath") — is a foundational self-regulation skill. Dad Village schedules this alongside curated video content built specifically for naming feelings.
